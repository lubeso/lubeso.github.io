Introduction

The history of calculus

The foundations of modern calculus are commonly attributed to two European scholars from the 17th century: Isaac Newton and Gottfried Leibniz. They worked independently of each other, and arrived to similar conclusions by different means: Newton used the theory of limits, whereas Leibniz used the theory of infinitesimals. Leibniz's methods were easier to use, but they lacked rigorous proof — evidence their results were correct. Mathematicians of the time would more quickly develop supporting evidence for Newton's methods, causing Leibniz's methods to lose formal priority and relevance.

In the 1960s, mathematician Abraham Robinson developed rigorous proof for Leibniz's infinitesimal calculus methods. Using concepts from mathematical logic, Robinson formally defined the hyperreal number system, an extension of the real number system which includes infinitesimally small and infinitely large numbers. Equipped with supporting evidence, we can now use the infinitesimal method to learn and apply differential and integral calculus.

Why infinitesimal calculus?

Calculus is traditionally taught using the theory of limits; what are the benefits of the theory of infinitesimals? Infinitesimals are easier to understand and use, as the fundamental insights for differential and integral calculus can be derived using comparatively simple mathematics concepts. The remainder of this article will demonstrate this claim by using the theory of infinitesimals to solve the following two problems:

  1. finding the slope of the curve at a specific point
  2. finding the area under the curve over a closed interval

Both problems will reference the following function:

Differential calculus

Problem 1

Find the slope of the function \(y = x^2\) at the point \((1, 1)\).

The slope of a function at a specific point is equal to the slope of the tangent line at that point. We can calculate the slope of the tangent line by first calculating the slope of a secant line and then bringing the second point infinitesimally close to the desired point. Using the hyperreal number system, we can write a general equation for this process: \[ \dfrac{\Delta y}{\Delta x} = \dfrac{f(x + \delta) - f(x)}{\delta} \]

In this equation, \(\delta\) represents an infinitesimal, a number greater than 0 but smaller than any other real number. Substituting our function \(y = x^2\) and our point of interest \((1,1)\), we arrive at the following calculation: \[ \begin{eqnarray} \dfrac{\Delta y}{\Delta x} =& \dfrac{\left(1 + \delta\right)^2 - 1^2}{\delta} \\ =& \dfrac{\left(1 + 2\delta + \delta^2\right) - 1}{\delta} \\ =& 2 + \delta \\ \end{eqnarray} \]

The slope must be a real number, so we can take the real part of the above calculation, and our final result is \(\tfrac{\Delta x}{\Delta y} = 2\). You can verify this result with the following interactive plot, adjusting the value of \(\delta\) and seeing how the approximation changes.

Integral calculus

Problem 2

Find the area under the function \(y = x^2\), between \(x = 1\) and \(x = 2\).

We can reconstruct the region \(A\) as the composite of infinitely many rectangles with width \(w = \delta\) and height \(h_i = (1 + i\delta)^2\). Using this definition, the area of region \(A\) is equal to the sum of each individual rectangle area: \[ A = \sum_{i=1}^{N} \left(w \cdot h_i\right) \] In this equation, \(N = 1 / \delta\) is the total number of thin rectangles; in the hyperreal number system, \(N\) represents an infinite number, a number greater than all other real numbers. More importantly, we can apply the same algebraic operations on \(N\) and \(\delta\) as with real numbers, and therefore we can decompose the sum as follows: \[ \begin{eqnarray} A =& \sum_{i=1}^{N} \left(\delta \cdot \left(1 + i\delta\right)^2\right) \\ =& \sum_{i=1}^{N} \left\{\delta \cdot \left[1 + 2i\delta + \left(i\delta\right)^2\right]\right\} \\ =& \sum_{i=1}^{N} \left(\delta + 2i\delta^2 + i^2\delta^3\right) \\ =& \delta \sum_{i=1}^{N} 1 + 2\delta^2 \sum_{i=1}^{N} i + \delta^3 \sum_{i=1}^{N} i^2\\ =& N\delta + 2\delta^2\left(\tfrac{N\left(N + 1\right)}{2}\right) + \delta^3\left(\tfrac{N(N + 1)(2N + 1)}{6}\right) \\ =& 1 + \delta^2\left(N^2 + N\right) + \tfrac{\delta^3}{6}\left(2N^3 + 3N^2 + N\right) \\ =& 1 + \left(1 + \delta\right) + \tfrac{1}{6}\left(2 + 3\delta + \delta^2\right) \\ =& \tfrac{7}{3} + \tfrac{3}{2}\delta + \tfrac{1}{6}\delta^2 \end{eqnarray} \]

The area must be a real number, so we take the real part of the above calculation as we did before, and our final result is \(A = \tfrac{7}{3}\). You can verify this result with the following interactive plot, adjusting the value of \(\delta\) and seeing how the approximation changes.

Conclusion

Although the result for the second problem required more algebraic manipulation than the first, we solved both using simple mathematics concepts. However, our solutions depend on the validity of the hyperreal number system, otherwise we could not apply the same algebraic operations on infinitesimally small and infinitely large quantities as we do with real numbers. Limits are the conventional alternative for formally defining properties of these quantities, but they are relatively more complicated.

In the case of differential calculus, the general equation for the slope of a function is similar but strange: \[ \frac{\Delta y}{\Delta x} = \lim_{h\to0}\dfrac{f(x + h) - f(x)}{h} \] This definition is strange, because when \(h = 0\), then \(\Delta y/\Delta x = 0/0\), which is undefined. Substituting our equation and point from before, we would ultimately arrive at the same result, but the reasoning is abstracted behind the idea of \(h\) approaching, but never quite reaching, zero.

In the case of integral calculus, the principles are the same, but without a valid representation of \(N\), the limit method requires computing an infinite sum: \[ A = \sum_{i=1}^{\infty} \left(\Delta x \cdot \left(1 + i\Delta x\right)^2\right) \\ \] With either method the result is the same, but for the method of limits this stage requires defining the properties of infinite series, such as convergence and divergence — comparatively more complicated than the infinitesimal method.

Calculus has important applications across science and engineering, and therefore calculus is a branch of mathematics worth learning. In honor of the importance of calculus, educational resources should present the topic using clear and intuitive methods. Pursuing this goal can require deviating from convention, but such is par the course for innovating and discovery.
