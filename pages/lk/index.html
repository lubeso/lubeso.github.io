Vision: Equity

Our mission is to develop and foster an equitable and welcoming living and learning community at MIT. We want students of all backgrounds and identities to feel respected and represented at every level of MIT, from the undergraduate body to the administration.

Areas of focus

I.   Admin and Student Interaction

The events of this semester have highlighted the importance of student input and consideration in administrative action. Across many of the newly developed policies regarding student life (housing, dining, academics, etc.), it feels as though the administration does not have our interests at heart, which is exacerbated by lack of transparency and dialogue.

Our goal is to ensure the Institute adequately reflects the values and voices of its students. This means:

  • Increased student representation and involvement in administrative policy development
  • Promoting and facilitating dialogue between the UA and the undergraduate body.
  • Ensuring campus resources are accountable to us as students
  • Enforcing a standard of transparency between the administration and student body.

II.   The Undergraduate Experience

Although we consistently rank among the top 5 universities in the nation, our undergraduate life is remarkably different from our peer institutions. Our undergraduate programs are world-class, but our lives as college students should not focus exclusively on academics. We need to prioritize student happiness and emotional fulfillment and combat our pervasive stress culture.

Our goal is to make undergraduate life an enjoyable and multifaceted experience. This means

  • Bolstering student support services, including resources for underrepresented minority, first-generation, and low-income students
  • Ensuring all undergraduate life offices prioritize student well-being, and actively reach out to students for feedback and input
  • Having staff and faculty participate in improving the student experience, including: public forum discussion, committees/coalitions of students, staff, and faculty
  • Promoting spaces on campus for students to lounge and hang out, perform, communicate, etc.
It is our goal that if a student has any opinion or concern regarding the undergraduate experience, they should not forfeit having their voice heard because of a belief that the feedback process is not worth their time or energy.

I.   Representation & Transparency

Student organizations/committees (DormCon, FSILGs, UA) have to independently and preemptively prepare a response to administrative action. They are left to their own devices, and must reach out to individual offices and resources (e.g. Housing and Residential Services) which are not always sufficiently cooperative. Therefore, students have to navigate a disjoint bureaucratic process to communicate concern, questions, and alternatives to admin, which may not be fully incorporated into final decisions.

Having students compile an informed response to an administrative policy is important, but it should not be their "burden of proof" in order to have their voices heard. Students often feel blindsided by admin's announcements, and have a restricted timeframe to propose any changes.

The Institute and Faculty committees provide a starting framework to incorporate student opinion, but we must make communication between committees, the UA, and the undergraduate body more effective. Having student representation is important, as well as presenting the whole gamut of student concerns and opinions. Our goal is to adapt these committees into an extension of the student body, and bolster the role of student feedback and suggestion in administrative policymaking.

As student representatives, we will interact with the student body directly with town halls and public forums, and meetings with student organizations (from ASAs to living groups). Representing the student body requires understanding their needs and concerns, which we will accomplish through active engagement. Our goal is to empower students to advocate for themselves, and hold us accountable to wholly representing their values. We are spokespeople for our constituents, but their voice is just as important as ours. Increasing collaboration in student response and policy making will ensure admin is presented with a holistic student perspective.

The UA is one of many processes involved in decision and policy making within the Institute's administration. However, it is the primary avenue for direct student input. Our goal is to highlight student voice and opinion to shape a better MIT. We want to make every component of the UA accessible to students through email to encourage more casual and frequent dialogue.

By establishing a clear path of communication between students, the UA, and campus resources and admin, we will promote accountability both ways. Students will remain up-to-date about developing administrative decisions, and admin will be directly informed of its students' input. Change should lie in the hands of students.

II. Institutional Accountability

The uncertainty surrounding MIT's COVID-19 response severely inhibited everyone's ability to prepare for any given outcome. Students relied on disjoint pieces of information from different sources—from department faculty to area directors. The proliferation of rumors with unverified credibility produced anxiety among the student body. Once sent out, the overdue email confirmed students' fears: we needed to vacate campus in the span of seven days. We erupted, in a variety of ways.

That Thursday, students organized to protest outside of the Division of Student Life (DSL) office. In response, Building 4 was temporarily closed off and when reopened, the door to DSL was locked, with a piece of printer paper taped to the window:

No particular office wants to be the focus of a student demonstration, but they must acknowledge and assuage student frustrations before they precipitate into protest. Appropriately supporting students requires continually embracing feedback and criticism, and incorporating our opinions at every stage of the decision-making process. Campus resources' only metric of efficacy ought to be student satisfaction, and so each office must be held accountable to us as students.

It's difficult to know where to improve when you're not actively looking. Meeting with Michael Bergren, he said: "We are always interested in improving; not remaining content with a plateau." To improve contributions to student life, offices must engage directly with the students whose lives they affect. UROP evaluations and student surveys are useful, but we can collect student opinion in many more ways. Our initiatives include:

  • Increased outreach: In order for Institute resources to enhance their impact and efficacy, they should consider on the students they don't reach. By understanding the access barriers to campus resources, we can better support all students.
  • Student committees: From offices to academic departments, having a representative body comprised of students will facilitate discussion surrounding student issues.
  • Student involvement: The purpose of these committees is to have student voice and perspective at the discussion table throughout our campus. Committees will keep students up-to-date with their proceedings, and campus resources will better understand our needs.

MIT is a collaborative environment—when we work together, students develop meaningful connections with each other, but mostly as a result of mutual suffering. We should pride ourselves in how happy our undergraduates are, not how much work they can push out.

I.   Students, Stress, & Support

The normalization of stressful academic experiences has consequences beyond sacrifices to our health. Stress culture inhibits our ability to be emotionally available and empathic individuals—ultimately affecting our interpersonal interactions within and without the MIT community.

We strive to create an environment where students put their health and well-being first without guilt or believing they are 'not working hard enough.' We want students to be able to develop holistically—our campus houses the brilliant minds of the future, and we cannot deprive them of a fulfilling social and academic college experience. Our goal is to provide students the resources to develop a strong sense of self, so they may realize their immense potential (and responsibility) to improve our society.

We want to improve and increase student support services for everyone, so that students can proactively care for their well-being and academic success. Our initiatives begin with making our Student Support Services (S3) office more robust, with more deans and venues for student contact. S3 currently staffs seven deans for 4,530 undergraduate students—but staffing more deans is not our only goal.

Meeting times are available by appointment or during daily walk-in hours (10-11am,2-3pm). We characterize our MIT experience by always being busy, without a minute to waste. With a demanding workload, we often perform a cost-benefit analysis of meeting with a dean or just pushing through this week's hump. Student access to support should not be restricted to time-sheets, since our lives extend far beyond the time and space of classes. Students should be able to contact support services through email to schedule meetings, request extensions, or simply check-in. Self-care should not be a chore.

Particularly important for our agenda is improving support for underrepresented minority (URM), first-generation, and low-income (FGLI) students. Students from these backgrounds carry unique struggles throughout their journey at MIT, and often go unnoticed in the development of effective insitutional policy. MIT prides itself in the diversity of students it admits, but its support infrastructure for URM and FGLI students is underdeveloped. The COVID-19 response has put on display how our students are supported—clearer now more than ever is the reality that not all students struggle equally.

Better supporting URM and FGLI students requires promoting (and funding) resources like SPXCE, the First Generation Program, and the Office of Minority Education, and requiring a Low-Income office. These offices are invaluable in supporting students from underprivileged backgrounds, and deserve robust funding and preservation. We will work closely with the FGLI community to understand what additional support services they require, and advocating with them at full force.

Furthermore, we want to provide physical campus spaces for these groups (like SPXCE, Latino Cultural Center, Rainbow Lounge, Black Student Union). We also want to hire more trained individuals throughout departments, program offices, and campus spaces to support students from underrepresented backgrounds. Providing URM and FGLI students appropriate representation and resources will directly address the disparity in living experiences on (and off) MIT's campus.

II.   Designing the Academic Experience

From where does our stress culture arise? Perhaps a reputation as a world-premier research institution begets strenuous, rigorous academics. But when we look at our peer institutions, their students appear relaxed and happy—nowhere near as stressed as we. With the world's leading scholars as instructors, why is the sentence "I have no idea what's going on in this class" so common (even comical?)

The student learning experience falters when it is not the 'primary focus' of faculty—that is, faculty are at the Institute primarly for research with teaching being closer to a contingency. When teaching staff receive negative course evaluations, there's no assurance the course will improve or change. Ensuring a reliable and enjoyable course is further inhibited when course instructors are different upon each offering.

Students' voices are often left out when developing course structure and pedagogy, but are most affected by the resultant negative teaching/learning experiences. Despite how intense our academic programs are, we are fully capable of enduring and overcoming the course difficulty. Every student at MIT has an incredible potential to succeed academically, but we should use that potential to understand and enjoy our courses instead of focusing on just staying afloat.

Our goal is to ensure students are the focus of undergraduate education. Students need to have a say in their learning experiences. Students should be comfortable and confident suggesting alternative course metholodogies and course structures. When test averages come out to 40/120, course instructors must engage in active dialogue with their students about how they felt during the exam, and how confident they feel in their command of the course content.

Our initiatives include:

  • Student, staff, and faculty coalitions: these coalitions will be present in all academic departments, and focus on discussing student experiences, to promote mutual understanding and advocacy.
  • Reshaping advising: Advising is one of the most important venues for students to navigate their field of interest and seek support from faculty, and yet not everyone has positive experiences with their advisor (some hardly ever meet with them.) Our goal is to have students share their suggestions for an optimal advising experienes, and train faculty on the importance of an advisor role.
  • Course check-ins: throughout the semester (and especially after 'bad' exam results), we want course instructors and staff to meet with students to discuss their concerns and opinions on the course. Whether in a class setting or by appointing students to reach out to their classmates, we believe feedback should not wait for course evaluations.

III.   Student Recreation

When we look at different parts of campus, it is clear the design objective was functionality, not necessarily aesthetic appeal. A university's quality does not depend exclusively on whether its architecture is Mediterranean or Renaissance, but the student experience is (literally) shaped by the physical environment we inhabit. When the Boston winters arrive, a gloomy campus appearance weighs all the more heavily on our students.

MIT's campus is not quite like our peer universities'. Where other campuses abound in leisure spaces, performance halls, and greenspace, we have labs, lecture halls, and meeting rooms. Our campus is certainly a place to get work done, but not necessarily to live as youth.

Stress culture structures our social interactions and expectations, with the partial renovation of the fifth floor of the Student Center (affectionally nicknamed "Stud 5") as an example. On Stud 5, students certainly study and work on psets, but there is a social component involved. We go to work with friends, and socialize with those who we see walking in. Sometimes we know we won't get work done, but we're happy to spend time with our friends—but social interaction in Stud 5 is predicated on the agreement that "we're going to get work done."

We need the space and opportunity to engage socially, talk about our hopes and dreams, lounge and hang out, and perform the arts to our hearts content. MIT' global reputation provides us an immense opportunity change and lead the world. But effective leadership requires social prowess and deep empathy, not just technical proficiency.

Our goal is to develop physical spaces on campus for undergraduates to socialize and celebrate their experiences as MIT students. Although campus real estate is limited, we want to work with both students and administration to craft a timeline of campus structural changes. Outdoor basketball courts and quads, building redesigns and additions, our goal is to create an MIT where students can live and work without sacrifice.

Our lives are deeper than the academic workloads we take on, and our Institute needs to provide us the infrastructure and resources for a multifacted and rich undergraduate experience.
