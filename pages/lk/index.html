Vision: Equity

Our mission is to develop and foster an equitable and welcoming living and learning community at MIT. We want students of all backgrounds and identities to feel respected and represented at every level of MIT, from the undergraduate body to the administration.

Areas of focus

I.   Admin and Student Interaction

The events of this semester have highlighted the importance of student input and consideration in administrative action. Across many of the newly developed policies regarding student life (housing, dining, academics, etc.), it feels as though the administration does not have our interests at heart, which is exacerbated by lack of transparency and dialogue.

Our goal is to ensure the Institute adequately reflects the values and voices of its students. This means:

  • Increased student representation and involvement in administrative policy development
  • Ensuring campus resources are accountable to us as students
  • Enforcing a standard of transparency between the administration and student body.

II.   The Undergraduate Experience

Although we consistently rank among the top 5 universities in the nation, our undergraduate life is remarkably different from our peer institutions. Our undergraduate programs are world-class, but our lives as college students should not focus exclusively on academics. We need to prioritize student happiness and emotional fulfillment and combat our pervasive stress culture.

Our goal is to make undergraduate life an enjoyable and multifaceted experience. This means

  • Bolstering student support services, including resources for underrepresented minority, first-generation, and low-income students
  • Ensuring all undergraduate life offices prioritize student well-being, and actively reach out to students for feedback and input
  • Having staff and faculty participate in improving the student experience, including: public forum discussion, committees/coalitions of students, staff, and faculty
  • Promoting spaces on campus for students to lounge and hang out, perform, communicate, etc.
Overall, we want to promote the importance of student input and advocacy for a better MIT. Everything from administrative policy to course structure affects our experiences as students, so we need to ensure our best interests and values are wholly represented!

1.1   Student Involvement

Student organizations/committees (DormCon, FSILGs, UA) have to independently and preemptively prepare a response to administrative action. They are left to their own devices, and must reach out to individual offices and resources (e.g. Housing and Residential Services) which are not always sufficiently cooperative. Therefore, students have to navigate a disjoint bureaucratic process to communicate concern, questions, and alternatives to admin, which may not be fully incorporated into final decisions.

Having students compile an informed response to an administrative policy is important, but it should not be their "burden of proof" in order to have their voices heard. Students often feel blindsided by admin's announcements, and have a restricted timeframe to propose any changes.

The Institute and Faculty committees provide a starting framework to incorporate student opinion, but interaction between committees, the UA, and the undergraduate body is not robust enough. Having student representation is important, as well as presenting the whole gamut of student concerns and opinions.

Our goal is to adapt these committees into an extension of the student body. Instead of a constrained interaction between admin and students, student representatives will engage frequently with the undergraduate body, through public forum discussion, meeting with living and learning communities/committees, and with individual students. Increasing collaboration in student response and policy making will ensure admin is presented with a holistic student perspective, and promote accountability both ways. Students will remain up-to-date about developing administrative decisions, and admin will be directly informed of its students' input.

1.2   Institutional Accountability

On March 19th, students organized to protest outside of the Division of Student Life(DSL) office. In response, Building 4 was temporarily closed off and when reopened, the door to DSL was locked, with a piece of printer paper taped to the window:

No particular office wants to be the focus of a student demonstration, but they must acknowledge and assuage student frustrations before they precipitate into protest. Appropriately supporting students requires continually embracing feedback and criticism, and incorporating our opinions at every stage of the decision-making process. Campus resources' only metric of efficacy ought to be student satisfaction, and so each office must be held accountable to us as students.
