April 13th, 2020

Design & Manufacturing I (2.007) is a mechanical engineering course where students design and develop a functional robot over the spring semester. The course culminates in a tournament, where students earn points based on how effectively their robot completes a task (or multiple tasks.) 2.007 is a critical, hands-on component of the MechE curriculum where students implement their understanding of mechanics, dynamics, and systems through computer-aided design, prototyping and manufacturing.

For a course like 2.007, a transition to online instruction severely disrupts its ability to achieve its learning objectives. But not all courses suffer equally; in particular, computer science courses within the EECS department already have significant online infrastructure. Courses like Fundamentals of Programming (6.009) involve server-side course handling where students test their code and upload their assignments and exams.

It is certainly a benefit that MIT has the technological infrastructure to move its courses online in this time of crisis. But course curricula and pedagogy should be developed around students' learning experience, not scalability.

The academic experience at MIT is intense, insofar as it pervades social interaction and expectation. For example, the term hosed is a popular piece of lexicon, and refers to being overwhelemed with work. Whether in passing or while catching up on each other's weeks, students almost always mention how stressed they are. But this runs deeper than a phrase.

When asked what we enjoy about MIT, the collaborative nature of the undergraduate body is often mentioned. We readily support one another; we'll stop what we're doing to help someone else. Working together, students develop meaningful connections with each other, but mostly as a result of mutual suffering.

Shared experiences of stress are what make MIT feel less competitive and cut-throat than its peer institutions. Working late into the night and occasionally pulling all-nighters, students are frustrated but vulnerable together. Compassion and trust come easily when you see another person as sleep-deprived and disheveled as you.

Though occasionally formative for friendships, the normalization of stress culture is detrimental towards students' overall social development. A preliminary example is how academic stress becomes a generic response when asked "how are you feeling?" When mutual empathy is constrained to an academic context, incorporating our emotions and personal struggles feels like an awkward maneuver. A façade so ubiquitous, we eventually believe it ourselves.

When stress becomes a primary characteristic of the undergraduate experience, we begin to dismiss recognizably negative behaviours—lack of sleep, missing a meal, mental and physical fatigue, these are all par for the course at MIT. When we observe these patterns in our peers, we are supportive and encourage them to seek help whenever they need it; but realizing the same for ourselves is not so easy.

Our Student Support Services (S3) is a small office, which can suggest that most MIT undergraduates don't need to consult support services. As a result, students may continue to brush off their own concerns as unimportant, and won't visit S3 until they are in significant distress. Student support should be proactive, not reactive.

From where does this stress arise? Perhaps a reputation as a world-premier research institution begets strenuous, rigorous academics. But when we look at our peer institutions, their students appear relaxed and happy—nowhere near as stressed as we. With the world's leading scholars as instructors, why is the sentence "I have no idea what's going on in this class" so common (even comical?)

MIT's 'elite'1 reputation is one contributing factor to a rocky undergraduate experience. STEM as a whole has a history of inaccess particularly as it relates to socioeconomic status. MIT Admissions' wonderful work in admitting students from underprivileged backgrounds is a preliminary step towards equity in higher education, but MIT's student body has been comprised primarily of privileged individuals with access to impeccable educational programs. As a result, privileged backgrounds are the most (historically) represented and recognized at the Institute. When one student has never seen differential calculus and another took multivariable calculus in high school, learning experiences will vary significantly—especially when professors make offhand remarks such as "now I know you've probably all seen this before…"

1 Elite institutions are created for elite minds, but the notion of an 'elite mind' implies a metric system for determining individual's deservingness to 'belong' to those same institutions. This point will be discussed in future issues.

The student learning experience falters when it is not the 'primary focus' of faculty—that is, faculty are at the Institute primarly for research with teaching being closer to a contingency. When teaching staff receive negative course evaluations, there's no assurance the course will improve or change. Ensuring a reliable and enjoyable course is further inhibited when course instructors are different upon each offering. One example is Linear Algebra (18.06), which has drastically different pedagogy and student response depending on who is the instructor.

Students' voices are often left out when developing course structure and pedagogy, but are most affected by the resultant negative teaching/learning experiences. Despite how intense our academic programs are, we are fully capable of enduring and overcoming the course difficulty. Every student at MIT has an incredible potential to succeed academically, but we should use that potential to understand and enjoy our courses instead of focusing on just staying afloat.

2.086 is notoriously unpopular amongst Course 2's—I also had a tough time in the course, but I ultimately enjoyed the topics I learned. When my friends complained to me about the course, I met with the professor and offered to gauge students" feelings, suggestions and concerns. They happily accepted, and together we restructed the course around student preferences.

Students need to have a say in their learning experiences. Students should be comfortable and confident suggesting alternative course metholodogies and course structures. When test averages come out to 40/120, course instructors must engage in active dialogue with their students about how they felt during the exam, and how confident they feel in their command of the course content. We should pride ourselves in how happy our undergraduates are, not how much work they can push out.
