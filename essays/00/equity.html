Navigating Equity in a Pandemic

April 6th, 2020

Our behavior as human beings is predicated on believing time is an infinite resource. We preoccupy ourselves with dates and deadlines, hoping our predictions into the future are not disrupted by unforeseen circumstances. Our outlook is bold and naive---time may last forever, but today will not. What reassurance is there in tomorrow if we care not for today?

With the outbreak of COVID-19, the luxury of tomorrow is no longer possible. The nature of this pandemic demands an immediate and effective response. Though we are all at risk of contracting the virus, not everyone can afford to work from home or self-isolate. With a world in crisis, how effectively do our institutions protect everyone?

On March 10th, MIT required all undergraduate students to vacate campus by the following Tuesday. As the week drew on, storage containers appeared throughout Memorial Drive, boxes filled the lobbies of residence halls; loss loomed heavily over MIT's campus. On Thursday at 23:00, an email was sent to undergraduate students requiring them to leave by Sunday.

Commisserating the unexpected end to a spring semester on-campus was not the only concern on students' minds. International students were uncertain if they would be eligible for reentry for the fall semester. Some students don't have enough money to fund a trip back home. Some students have at-risk family members living at home. Some students' return home would pose a significant financial stress on their household. Some students are afraid of going home. Some students don't have a home.

The administration provided two primary forms of support:

  1. travel reimbursements of up to $500
  2. a request form to stay on campus

Students became frustrated with the lack of transparency as it was unclear what made a student eligible to stay on-campus, coupled with callous responses of 'alternatives' to consider instead of remaining at MIT. Students organized to protest outside the Division of Student Life office, but prior to the event Building 4 was blocked off. Once reopened, the DSL office door was locked, with a piece of letter paper attached to the window:

Perhaps they wanted to avoid contact with such a large group of demonstrators, or maybe they worried how aggressive the students would be. Whatever the case, the office had shut its doors to the frustrated students it was meant to support. Why was the office so unprepared for such a response from the student body?

As an elite institution, MIT carries a distinct history of inaccessability. Women comprised only 29% of the admitted class of 1988. The Racial Climate Report of 1986 found that minorities felt isolated and their bar for entry was lower than their white peers. Over the past ten years, African-American/Black enrollment has increased by 1%.

Resources and offices for minority, first-generation, and low-income students are recent developments in MIT's history, and most of their advancement is accomplished by the offices themselves and/or student advocacy. Elite universities were designed for a particular subset of the population: those sufficiently affluent to afford a private education, a haughty price tag in exchange for prestige. As a result, MIT and its peer institutions are best equipped to address the needs and concerns of the elite.

A history of inequity is particularly difficult to shed, reflected by an extensive (and divisive) debate amongst undergraduates concerning emergency grading policy. Section 2.64 of MIT's Rules & Regulations for Faculty Governance mandated an emergency grading system of pass/no record in response to a 'severe disruption of the semester.' In the midst of a pandemic, some students proposed an 'opt-in' alternative, in which students could choose to receive letter grades if they desired.

These students presented the argument that a mandatory P/NR model would be detrimental for post-graduate admissions, future internships and jobs, and GPA-dependent scholarships and programs. For these students, an opt-in model would certainly relieve their concerns.

However, the existence of this debate highlights two characteristics of MIT's culture:

  1. Work and grades are a greater priority than wellbeing
  2. An underprivileged background is not part of the 'quintessential' MIT student

Although inequity does not disappear when on MIT's campus, the severity and scope is constrained to an MIT-context. In addition, students have access to campus resources, friends, and faculty for support. Once off campus, that safety net is removed, and the disparity in living quality varies significantly.

Now, students may live in households of 2 to 8+ members, students may have to take care of family members, students may be homeless or have insecure housing, students' families may be facing food insecurity, students' families may be facing significant economic turmoil due to the outbreak, the list goes on. These types of inequalities are not the same as those experienced on-campus, and MIT's ability to mitigate their severity is reduced significantly.

For these students, grades are no longer the primary focus of their lives. An opt-in model is forcing a choice of sacrifice: their wellbeing, or future career prospects. Fortunately, the emergency grading policy will remain as-is.

There are many ways in which MIT could have better supported its underrepresented students through this situation. But a list of hypotheticals cannot calm the unnerving reality that these students are not the primary concern in the development and aftermath of administrative action. Beyond being a minority at MIT, our identities are unperceived, invisible in the eyes of our Institute.

But such a reality does not mean hope is futile; the events of this spring semester indicate the need for structural and cultural change at every level of the Institute. Students are facing tangible realities, and the administration is more than capable in supporting us. Solutions can better address the needs of students if they are developed though transparent dialogue between the undergraduate body and the administration.

We cannot know joy without pain, and during this global crisis I have witnessed how we can come together as an MIT community. My friends and family at MIT have taught me perserverance and compassion, and my only wish is for the Institute to appropriately reflect and care for the resilient student body it has.
